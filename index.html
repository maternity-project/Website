The EquiBirth study

Tackling avoidable health differences during pregnancy

Project A now recruiting

Why is the project needed?

All birthing people and babies deserve health that is as good as the groups with the best health.

Sadly, this is not currently the case in the UK.

The structure of our society and healthcare system means that people from the following groups are at higher risk of poor health before, during and after pregnancy:

  • People who are from minority racial or ethnic groups
  • People who have less money or resources
  • People with lower levels of education
  • People whose gender or sex differs from their gender or sex at birth
  • People who are lesbian, gay or bisexual
  • People who are seeking asylum or who have migrated from another country
  • People who do not speak English
  • People who are homeless
  • People who use recreational drugs
  • People who have additional needs

People from these groups have been shown to be more likely to:

  • Develop high blood pressure or diabetes (high blood sugar levels) in pregnancy
  • Have their baby early i.e., before 37 completed weeks of pregnancy
  • Experience a stillbirth (where the baby dies in the womb before birth), or for their baby to die in the period after birth
  • Experience severe tears at the time of birth
  • More likely (although still rare) for the birthing person to die during pregnancy or in the year after birth

What does the project involve?

The first part of the project has three parts:

Part 1

Creation of a model (or picture) that explains why and how avoidable health differences arise

Part 2

A Prioity Setting Partnership in association with the James Lind Alliance.

This involves asking women, and the health professionals working with them, what questions they want research to answer.

This helps to ensure that future research addresses the questions and unknowns that are the most important to you.

Part 3

Before we can tackle avoidable health differences we need to be able to measure them.

Part 3 brings birthing people, health professionals and researchers together to decide what we should measure and how.
